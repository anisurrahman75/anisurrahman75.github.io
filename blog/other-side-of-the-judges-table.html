The Other Side of the Judge's Table

By Anisur Rahman · Jul 2026

Between 2019 and 2022 I solved over two thousand programming problems across Codeforces, AtCoder, UVa, and SPOJ. The scoreboard version of that story: a 2195 peak rating on CodeChef, Expert on Codeforces, and an Honorable Mention with team DIU_Sedulous at the ICPC Asia Dhaka Regional. But the more useful education started when I switched sides.

Solving is easy; specifying is hard

As a competitor, the problem statement is a fixed universe: read it, find the trick, implement fast. As a problem setter — first for DIU's Take-Off contest in 2021, then for Unlock the Algorithm and several rounds after — you own the universe, and every ambiguity in it becomes someone's wasted hour.

Writing a good statement turned out to be specification work. What exactly happens at n = 0? Are duplicate elements allowed? Is the graph guaranteed connected, and does the answer even exist if it isn't? Competitors will find every gap you left, at scale, during a live contest, while the clock runs. There is no better training for writing API contracts.

Test data is adversarial systems design

The judge only accepts what your test data rejects. So a setter spends most of their time thinking like an attacker against their own reference solution: the quadratic solution that sneaks under the time limit unless you craft the worst case, the greedy that survives random tests and dies only on a hand-built chain, the off-by-one that needs exactly n = 1 to show itself.

Years later, writing chaos tests for backup systems felt oddly familiar. Generating the workload that breaks a distributed controller is the same craft as generating the test case that breaks a greedy heuristic — you are searching the input space for the corner where the invariant fails.

Judging in real time

By 2022 I was Judging Director for the preliminary round of DIU CSE Fest, and later served as Vice President of the ACM Wing of DIU's Computer & Programming Club. During a live contest the judge's side is an operations role: clarification requests arriving mid-round, a rejudge decision that affects forty teams, a wrong test file that must be fixed without leaking information. You learn to make careful decisions quickly, in public, with incomplete information — and to write the post-mortem afterward.

Why this still matters to me

Competitive programming gave me speed and pattern recognition. Setting and judging gave me something rarer: the habit of asking "what input would make this wrong?" before shipping anything. That question followed me into production systems, and it is the same question that drives the research I want to do — finding the corner cases where distributed systems quietly break their promises.
